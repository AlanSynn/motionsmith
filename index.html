Overview

Built to connect creative intent, mechanism design, and fabrication.

We present MotionSmith, a sketch-based computational design system developed through participatory design (PD) with three experienced makers of mechanical crafts (automata). MotionSmith enables users to sketch and iteratively identify a desired motion, explore system-generated mechanisms to realize the motion, refine the chosen mechanism, and export fabrication-ready files.

We developed the system through a year-long iterative process, including PD with three artists. Insights from this collaboration emphasized the importance of supporting creative intent alongside sketch fidelity, enabling fluent iteration across design stages, and ensuring mechanically simple, fabricable outcomes.

The resulting system leverages computational support while maintaining user agency across sketching, mechanism synthesis, and blueprint generation. Our deployment with experts shows how this workflow bridges user intent and mechanical realization and surfaces opportunities to expand educational scaffolding and fabrication guidance for a broader community of makers.

Demo

System

Three stages keep the workflow easy to follow.

MotionSmith moves from motion-path sketching to mechanism generation to fabrication export in one continuous pipeline.

01

Identify a goal

Import a character, define the rig, sketch a motion path, and adjust pacing or smoothness.

02

Generate mechanisms

Compare four-bar, cam-follower, and gear candidates, then refine the selected mechanism directly on canvas.

03

Support fabrication

Export SVG or PDF blueprint packets that bridge the digital design to a physical automaton.

Interface

One workspace for drawing, simulation, editing, and export.

The application keeps authoring, mechanism comparison, parametric refinement, pacing review, and blueprint export in one place, so the workflow stays continuous as the design becomes more concrete.

Cases

Three artist-led deployments show how the system holds up in practice.

Each deployment spans the full handoff from MotionSmith authoring, to mechanism choice, to fabrication and the finished automaton.

Case 01

Lu Lyu / Shy elephant

The piece evolved from a simpler leg lift into a quieter flower-holding gesture with a distinct shy rhythm.

  • Mechanism. LL chose a four-bar linkage because it supported a clear raise-pause-settle rhythm within the explored mechanism set.
  • Fabrication. MotionSmith SVG output was extruded in Fusion 360, then combined with 3D-printed linkages, cardstock layers, and brass fasteners.
  • Why it matters. The case shows how expressive intent and motion matching can be considered together during mechanism selection.

Case 02

Tom Haney / Cheering man

This deployment turned iterative arm-raising sketches into a cleaner, more humorous cheering gesture.

  • Mechanism. TH selected cams because they created a clear apex accent and supported the intended cheering gesture within the explored mechanism set.
  • Fabrication. MotionSmith blueprints became asymmetric paper cams, wire linkages, and wooden followers mounted in a synchronized frame.
  • Why it matters. The case highlights how simplicity, visual clarity, and trajectory accuracy can be balanced together.

Case 03

Marc Horovitz / Tin robot

The robot expanded from arm motion to a fuller angel-like gesture with coordinated arm and leg accents.

  • Mechanism. MH composed a compound cam using larger arm cams and smaller leg cams to balance presence, comprehensibility, and fabrication effort.
  • Fabrication. Printed blueprint packets were used as cutting guides for paper and wood parts before assembly on a backing board.
  • Why it matters. The case shows how MotionSmith can hand off most of the process even when multi-motion synthesis still needs manual completion.
